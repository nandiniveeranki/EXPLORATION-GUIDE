Butter Chicken (Murgh Makhani)

Butter Chicken, also known as Murgh Makhani, has its origins in Delhi during the 1950s. It was accidentally created by the chefs at the famous Moti Mahal restaurant, founded by Kundan Lal Gujral, Kundan Lal Jaggi, and Thakur Dass. The dish was born out of a need to avoid wasting leftover tandoori chicken, which tended to dry out. To preserve its flavor and moisture, the cooks simmered the chicken in a rich tomato-based gravy with butter, cream, and aromatic spices. This innovation not only revived the dry meat but also gave birth to a dish that quickly gained immense popularity. Over time, Butter Chicken became one of the most iconic dishes of North Indian cuisine, loved for its creamy texture, smoky flavor, and balance of spices. Today, it is celebrated worldwide as a symbol of Indian culinary tradition.

Rogan Josh

Rogan Josh is a signature dish of Kashmiri cuisine, with roots tracing back to the Mughal era when the Mughals brought Persian influences into India. The name comes from Persian words: ‚Äúrogan‚Äù meaning ‚Äúoil‚Äù or ‚Äúclarified butter‚Äù and ‚Äújosh‚Äù meaning ‚Äúintense heat‚Äù or ‚Äúpassion,‚Äù signifying the dish‚Äôs rich, hearty nature. Originally adapted from Persian lamb stews, Rogan Josh was refined in the Kashmir Valley, where it became part of the traditional Wazwan (a multi-course royal feast). Traditionally prepared with tender lamb or mutton, the curry is flavored with a blend of aromatic spices such as fennel, dry ginger, cardamom, cloves, and Kashmiri red chilies, which lend it a vibrant red color without overwhelming spiciness. Slow-cooked to perfection, the dish embodies the fusion of Persian techniques with Kashmiri flavors, making Rogan Josh a timeless culinary classic of India.

Dal Makhani

Dal Makhani is a beloved North Indian dish that originated in Punjab and is closely tied to the post-partition culinary innovations of the 20th century. It was popularized by the chefs of Moti Mahal restaurant in Delhi, the same place where Butter Chicken was created. Traditionally, lentils were a staple in Punjabi households, but the idea of enriching them with butter (makhan) and cream transformed the humble dish into a luxurious delicacy. Dal Makhani is made primarily from whole black lentils (urad dal) and red kidney beans (rajma), which are slow-cooked for hours to achieve a creamy, smoky flavor. The addition of butter, cream, and aromatic spices gave the dish its signature richness and velvety texture. Over time, Dal Makhani became an essential part of festive meals, weddings, and restaurant menus, symbolizing the perfect blend of rustic Punjabi flavors with modern culinary refinement. Today, it stands as one of the most iconic vegetarian dishes of Indian cuisine, enjoyed worldwide.

Chole Bhature

Chole Bhature is a classic North Indian dish that combines spicy chickpea curry (chole) with deep-fried leavened bread (bhature). Its origins are often traced to Punjab, though it has since become popular across India, especially in Delhi, where it is considered a staple street food and breakfast delicacy. The dish is believed to have gained prominence during the mid-20th century as urban eateries began pairing the protein-rich chickpea curry, flavored with onions, tomatoes, ginger, garlic, and a robust mix of spices, with the soft yet crispy bhature made from fermented wheat flour dough. The combination quickly became a favorite because it is both hearty and indulgent, symbolizing the richness of Punjabi cuisine. Over the years, Chole Bhature has become more than just a meal‚Äîit is an emotion for many food lovers, enjoyed at roadside dhabas, sweet shops, and restaurants alike, often served with pickles, onions, and a glass of lassi.

Rajma Chawal

Rajma Chawal is a comforting and iconic North Indian dish that holds a special place in Punjabi and Himalayan households. It consists of red kidney beans (rajma) slow-cooked in a thick, spiced tomato-onion gravy, served with steamed basmati rice (chawal). The dish was originally introduced to India from Mexico and Central America, as kidney beans are not native to the subcontinent. They were brought by the Portuguese and gradually adapted into Indian cuisine, especially in the northern regions where the climate of Jammu and Himachal Pradesh proved ideal for their cultivation. Over time, rajma was infused with Indian spices, giving birth to the flavorful curry we know today. Rajma Chawal became a staple of Punjabi households, loved for its simplicity, nutrition, and homely taste. Beyond being a wholesome meal, it carries an emotional connection‚Äîoften associated with comfort food, family gatherings, and Sunday lunches. Today, it remains one of the most popular vegetarian dishes across India.

Nihari

Nihari is a rich, slow-cooked stew that originated in the royal kitchens of Mughal India during the 18th century. The name comes from the Arabic word ‚ÄúNahar,‚Äù meaning morning, as it was traditionally eaten after the Fajr (dawn) prayer to provide warmth and strength throughout the day. Initially a breakfast dish for Mughal nobles in Delhi, Nihari soon spread to the common people and became especially popular among workers for its sustaining quality. The dish is usually made with beef or mutton shank, simmered overnight with aromatic spices such as cloves, cardamom, fennel, and nutmeg, resulting in tender meat and a deeply flavorful gravy. In many places, bone marrow (nalli) is added, enhancing the richness. After the partition of India in 1947, Nihari became a hallmark of Delhi‚Äôs Muslim cuisine as well as Pakistani culinary culture, particularly in Karachi and Lahore. Today, Nihari is enjoyed as a festive or special-occasion dish, symbolizing royal heritage, community, and culinary tradition.

Kashmiri Dum Aloo

Kashmiri Dum Aloo is a traditional and much-loved dish from the Kashmir Valley, celebrated for its vibrant flavors and cultural significance. The dish consists of baby potatoes that are first deep-fried until golden and then slow-cooked (dum) in a rich, spiced gravy. Unlike the creamy versions popularized in restaurants elsewhere, the authentic Kashmiri Dum Aloo is made with a base of yogurt, fennel, dry ginger, and Kashmiri red chili powder, which imparts its signature fiery red color without excessive heat. The slow-cooking technique allows the potatoes to absorb the aromatic flavors fully, resulting in a dish that is both hearty and delicately spiced. Traditionally part of the elaborate Kashmiri Wazwan (a royal multi-course feast), Dum Aloo holds a place of pride in local cuisine, especially during festivals and weddings. Its unique blend of simple ingredients with bold flavors reflects the essence of Kashmiri culinary artistry.

South Indian Cuisine

South India is famous for its dosas, idlis, sambar, and filter coffee. Coconut and tamarind are widely used. Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Kerala, and Andhra Pradesh are food hotspots.

Dose

South Indian tiffin refers to the light breakfast or evening meals that are popular in states like Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Telangana, Karnataka, and Kerala. These dishes are usually made from rice, lentils, or semolina and are light, healthy, and easy to digest. Common items include idli (steamed rice cakes), dosa (crispy rice-lentil crepe), uttapam (thick dosa with toppings), pongal (rice-lentil dish), upma (semolina preparation), and vada (fried lentil fritters). They are typically served with sambar (a lentil-based vegetable stew) and a variety of chutneys like coconut, tomato, or mint.

Pongal (Ven Pongal)

Ven Pongal, often just called ‚ÄúPongal,‚Äù is a traditional South Indian dish that is both a daily tiffin item and a festive preparation. It is made by cooking rice and moong dal (yellow split lentils) together until soft, then tempering with ghee, black pepper, cumin seeds, ginger, curry leaves, and cashews. The addition of ghee and spices not only enhances the flavor but also makes the dish aromatic and nourishing. Ven Pongal is considered a comfort food because of its smooth, porridge-like texture, making it light on the stomach yet filling. It is usually served with sambar and coconut chutney, which balance the richness of the dish. Beyond its role as a popular breakfast in Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, and Andhra Pradesh, Pongal holds deep cultural importance, especially during the Pongal harvest festival in Tamil Nadu, where it symbolizes prosperity, gratitude to nature, and the celebration of new harvests.

Hyderabadi Biryani

Hyderabadi Biryani is a world-renowned rice delicacy that represents the rich cultural and culinary heritage of Hyderabad. Introduced by the Mughals and later refined in the kitchens of the Nizams, it is a royal dish made by combining fragrant basmati rice with marinated meat such as mutton or chicken, cooked together with a blend of spices like cinnamon, cardamom, cloves, saffron, and fried onions. What makes it unique is the traditional dum cooking method, where the pot is sealed with dough to trap steam, allowing the rice and meat to slowly cook together while absorbing the spices, creating layers of aroma and flavor. The dish has two famous variations: Kacchi Biryani, where raw marinated meat is cooked along with rice, and Pakki Biryani, where pre-cooked meat is layered with rice. Hyderabadi Biryani is usually accompanied by mirchi ka salan, a tangy-spicy chili curry, and raita, which balances the heat of the dish. Beyond being a meal, it is considered a symbol of celebration and hospitality, often served at weddings, festivals, and special occasions, making it one of the most iconic and loved dishes of India.

Andhra Meals

Andhra meals are a traditional, wholesome thali-style cuisine from Andhra Pradesh, famous for their bold, spicy, and tangy flavors. Typically served on a banana leaf or a large plate, the meal revolves around steamed rice as the staple, accompanied by an array of dishes that bring variety and balance. Common items include pappu (dal cooked with leafy greens or vegetables), pulusu (tamarind-based curry), koora (dry or gravy vegetable curry), pachadi (chutneys made from vegetables, greens, or curd), sambar, and rasam. To enhance taste, crispy appadams (papads) and flavorful pickles such as the iconic Avakaya (spicy mango pickle) are always included. Andhra cuisine is especially noted for its generous use of red chilies and tamarind, giving the dishes a fiery yet tangy flavor that sets them apart from other South Indian meals. Non-vegetarian Andhra meals feature a variety of spicy chicken, mutton, fish, and prawn curries, which are deeply rooted in the region‚Äôs culinary traditions. The meal usually ends with perugu (curd rice), which helps cool down the palate after the spicy dishes. Andhra meals are not just about food‚Äîthey reflect the hospitality and cultural richness of the region, often served during festivals, weddings, and family gatherings, symbolizing abundance and warmth.

Payasam (Kheer)

Payasam, called Kheer in North India, is one of the most beloved and ancient Indian desserts, prepared across regions with slight variations but the same essence of sweetness and festivity. It is traditionally made by slow-cooking milk with a base ingredient such as rice, semolina (rava), or vermicelli (semiya) until it turns creamy, then sweetened with sugar or jaggery. To enhance its richness, it is flavored with cardamom, saffron, and ghee, and garnished with cashews, almonds, and raisins fried in ghee. In South India, Payasam is an integral part of festivals, temple offerings, and wedding feasts‚Äîespecially dishes like Pal Payasam (milk-rice version), Semiya Payasam (vermicelli version), and Ada Pradhaman (a Kerala specialty made with rice flakes and jaggery). In North India, Kheer is equally popular, often served during religious rituals and special occasions. Beyond being just a dessert, Payasam carries a symbolic meaning of prosperity, joy, and divine blessing, as it is often offered to deities before being shared with devotees. Its creamy texture, delicate sweetness, and fragrant spices make it not just a dish, but a cultural expression of celebration and togetherness.

Punugulu

Punugulu is a beloved South Indian snack, particularly famous in Andhra Pradesh and Telangana, and is commonly sold in street food stalls, tiffin centers, and homes. It is prepared using idli or dosa batter, which is slightly fermented to give a light tangy flavor, then shaped into small balls and deep-fried until golden brown. The result is a crispy, crunchy outer layer with a soft and fluffy inside. Punugulu is typically served with a variety of chutneys such as coconut chutney, peanut chutney, tomato chutney, or ginger chutney, and sometimes even with spicy powders like karam podi mixed with oil. Depending on preference, some versions may include chopped onions, green chilies, or curry leaves in the batter to enhance the taste. Easy to make and quick to fry, Punugulu is a popular evening snack enjoyed with tea, especially during the rainy season, and is also served at small gatherings and casual meals. It reflects the simple yet flavorful street food culture of Andhra and Telangana, turning leftover dosa/idli batter into a crispy, mouth-watering delicacy loved by all ages.

East Indian Cuisine

Known for sweets like Rasgulla and Sandesh, and fish-based curries. Bengali cuisine uses mustard oil and fresh water fish extensively.

Shorshe Ilish (West Bengal)

Shorshe Ilish is a traditional and iconic Bengali dish made with Hilsa fish (Ilish) cooked in a rich, tangy, and flavorful mustard sauce. The fish is marinated with turmeric and salt, then simmered in a paste of mustard seeds, green chilies, and mustard oil, which gives the dish its sharp and pungent taste. Litti Chokha (Bihar)

Litti Chokha is a traditional and rustic dish from the Indian states of Bihar and Jharkhand. It consists of litti, a roasted dough ball made from whole wheat flour and filled with sattu (roasted gram flour) mixed with spices, onions, garlic, and pickles. The litti is traditionally roasted over cow dung cakes, giving it a characteristic smoky flavor. The dish is served with chokha, a mash of roasted vegetables like potato, brinjal (eggplant), and tomato, seasoned with mustard oil, green chilies, and coriander. Litti Chokha is a complete and nutritious meal, often found at roadside stalls and local eateries. It embodies the essence of Bihari rural life, offering a wholesome and earthy taste.

Momos (Northeast)

Momos, originating from the Himalayan regions and widely popular in Northeast India, are a type of dumpling that have become a staple street food and household snack.
