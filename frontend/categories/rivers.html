Yamuna River

The Yamuna, a major tributary of the Ganga, originates from Yamunotri Glacier in Uttarakhand. Flowing through Delhi, Haryana, and Uttar Pradesh, it is vital for agriculture, industries, and drinking water supply to the capital region. The Taj Mahal stands on its banks at Agra. Spread across a length of about 1,376 km, the Yamuna supports diverse ecosystems, fertile agricultural plains, and major urban centers. Numerous pilgrimage sites, including Yamunotri, Mathura (the birthplace of Lord Krishna), and Vrindavan, are situated along its banks, attracting devotees from across the country. Efforts are underway to clean and rejuvenate the river through initiatives like the Yamuna Action Plan, focusing on improving water quality and restoring ecological balance. Despite these challenges, the Yamuna continues to be a symbol of spiritual heritage, ecological diversity, and economic sustenance for millions of people in India.

The Yamuna remains a symbol of India‚Äôs cultural heritage, natural beauty, and deep-rooted history, connecting millions of people through geography, livelihood, and faith.

The Brahmaputra holds immense cultural and spiritual significance for the indigenous communities of Northeast India, with numerous festivals, rituals, and folklore centered around the river. The river‚Äôs hydroelectric potential is being harnessed through various projects, contributing to regional energy needs.
